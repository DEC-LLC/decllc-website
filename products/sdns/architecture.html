Architecture

How it's built — without the wiring diagram.

You don't need to know how a car's transmission works to trust that it will get you home. You need to know the brakes are tested, the airbags are real, and the seatbelts aren't decorative. Here's what's built into [PRODUCT]-SDNS and why each piece matters.

The shape of the thing

If DEC-LLC's other products are domain experts, [PRODUCT]-SDNS is the place they all agree to meet.

A concert hall, not a command center

Picture a concert hall. The stage has a violin section, a brass section, a percussion section. Each section has its own experts, its own sheet music, its own way of playing. A conductor stands at the front. The conductor doesn't play any instrument. The conductor holds the score — the single agreed-upon piece of music — and gives cues.

[PRODUCT]-SDNS is the conductor's stand. The products on the stage are the sections. The score is your policy. The cues are the coordinated actions that happen across all of them.

[PRODUCT]-SDNS sits at the center as a coordination layer. Products remain experts in their own domains — [PRODUCT]-SDNS provides the shared policy, identity, naming, and audit that crosses between them.
No one section is "above" the others. The conductor holds the score, but the violinist still decides how to bow. The oboist still tunes the orchestra. [PRODUCT]-SDNS does not take over your other products — it holds the shared context they all agree to.

The five things [PRODUCT]-SDNS holds for you

Every coordination layer has to answer five questions. Here is how [PRODUCT]-SDNS answers each.

1. "Who are you?" — The directory

Every action across your infrastructure needs to answer: who is asking? Is it a human employee, a service account, a backup job, a visiting contractor? [PRODUCT]-SDNS is the single place that holds those answers. A user exists once, and every subordinate product trusts [PRODUCT]-SDNS to vouch for them.

Like the front desk of a building that issues one badge per person. Every door in the building respects that badge. You don't need a separate badge for the elevator, the parking garage, and the cafeteria.

2. "What is that thing called?" — The name service

Your printers, servers, switches, workstations, cameras, and VMs all have names. Those names mean things only if something authoritative says they do. [PRODUCT]-SDNS is that authority, for everything inside your infrastructure.

Like the post office for your building — not the public postal service. Internal mail knows which floor, which room, which person. External mail comes in, but inside the building, the routing is yours.

3. "Prove you are who you say you are." — The certificate authority

Every encrypted connection between your systems needs a way to verify the other end isn't an imposter. [PRODUCT]-SDNS issues and validates those proofs for your infrastructure — no external certificate authority required.

Like the notary public in your own office — your notary can stamp things as genuine without waiting for a government-appointed notary to visit. The stamps are trusted inside your organization because everyone in your organization trusts your notary.

4. "What are the rules?" — The policy store

The rules about who can do what, where, and when live in one place. Firewalls, switches, backup jobs, and VM hosts all read from the same rulebook — so a change applied once takes effect everywhere it needs to.

Like a building's master floor plan. One architect edits it. Every contractor works from the same copy. You don't have four contractors working from four different blueprints and discovering the discrepancies on demolition day.

5. "What happened?" — The audit log

Every change, every login, every policy edit, every certificate rotation is recorded — once, in one place, tamper-evident, timestamp-signed. When someone asks "who changed this on Tuesday?", the answer is a single query.

Like the elevator maintenance log that the inspector reads every year. Complete, chronological, signed. The inspector doesn't want four separate logs that might or might not agree with each other.

Architecturally safer, on purpose

These are the design decisions we made so that the coordination layer can't become the single point of compromise.

1

Sealed compartments

Every component inside [PRODUCT]-SDNS runs in its own sealed room. If one room catches fire, the others are untouched. A bug in the name service cannot reach the certificate authority. A compromised log reader cannot rewrite the policy store. (In shipbuilding, this is called "watertight bulkheads.")

2

Least keys, ever

Every service has a specific set of keys — not the master key. The name service can look up names. It cannot change certificates. The audit log can record events. It cannot approve them. The policy engine can issue rules. It cannot log in as a user. When a key is compromised, only the one door it opens is at risk.

3

Every request verified, every time

Inside the coordination layer, no component trusts any other component just because they share a building. Every request between services is verified — same standard as any request from outside. This is the architectural answer to an attacker who has already gotten one foot in the door.

4

Tamper-evident history

The audit log is built so that altering a past record is detectable. If an attacker breaks in and tries to erase their tracks, the erasure itself leaves a mark. The history can be checked against itself to prove it hasn't been edited.

5

Recovery without dependency

Every [PRODUCT]-SDNS appliance knows how to restore itself from a sealed recovery key that you — and only you — hold. No phone-home required. No support ticket. No vendor portal. If the appliance is wiped, you recover it from the envelope in your safe.

6

Updates that don't need the internet

Security updates can arrive from your own internal repository, from a USB key, or from a sealed update image. The appliance does not require cloud connectivity to stay current — which matters for air-gapped deployments, but also for anyone who remembers the last time their "required cloud" was down for six hours.

Where the trust lines are

Knowing where you have to trust the system and where you don't is half of security.

Everything inside the dashed line is yours. [PRODUCT]-SDNS doesn't pull in trust from outside vendors to function. If you disconnect from the internet, the coordination layer keeps running.

What's inside the line

Your [PRODUCT]-SDNS appliance, your subordinate product appliances, your employees, your policies, your audit records, your certificates, your name service. All of this runs on your hardware, under your SLA, in your building (or your cloud tenant, if that's where your datacenter lives).

What's outside the line

Public internet. Cloud identity providers. Public certificate authorities. Any service you don't own. By default, nothing outside the line is granted authority to change anything inside. If you choose to integrate with an external system, you do it deliberately — not by accident of vendor default settings.

Architecture is the quiet work.

The decisions on this page were made before the first line of code. They are the reason [PRODUCT]-SDNS can survive a compromise of one component, an internet outage, a vendor going dark, or an auditor arriving unannounced.
