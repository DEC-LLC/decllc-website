The Coordination Layer

One control plane for every system you run.

SDNS is the coordination layer for your entire infrastructure — networking, compute, security, backups, identity, certificates, and DNS — all managed from one place, on your hardware, under your authority.

See the Architecture Why Sovereign Matters

Think of it as the conductor of your infrastructure orchestra.

A conductor doesn't own the musicians. Doesn't play the instruments. Doesn't tell the violinist how to hold a bow. What the conductor does is coordinate: tempo, cues, entrances, exits — turning a room of independent virtuosos into one coherent performance.

That's what SDNS does for your infrastructure. Your NIVMIA watches the network. Your IVMIA manages your virtual machines. Your OpenUTM guards the perimeter. Your VaultSync protects your data. Each one is an expert in its own domain. SDNS is the one place you look to see them all — and the one place you act to change them all.

It is not a management platform that takes control away from your other tools. It is a coordination layer that lets them keep doing what they do best, while giving you a single pane of glass to orchestrate across them.

What "one control plane" actually means for you

Your infrastructure today probably looks like this: one dashboard for your firewall, another for your switches, another for VMware (if you still have it), another for backups, another for DNS, another for identity, and a text file somewhere of your TLS certificates. Every time something needs to change — a new user, a new service, a blocked IP, a rotated certificate — you log into several places and hope nothing falls through the cracks.

SDNS replaces that with one place. One login. One audit log. One place to define who can access what. One place to rotate certificates across every system simultaneously. One place to see the full state of your infrastructure — which ends the "whose job is it?" conversation permanently.

Why "sovereign" matters

Most management platforms are run by someone else. Your data goes to their cloud. Your identity system belongs to Microsoft or Google. Your certificate authority is maintained by a vendor who can (and does) change the rules on you. If they break, you break. If they go out of business, you rebuild.

SDNS is yours. It runs on your hardware. It doesn't phone home. It doesn't need the internet to function. If you unplugged your datacenter from the world tomorrow, your infrastructure would keep coordinating itself — because the coordination authority lives on your own floor.

What SDNS is responsible for

✓

Your directory

Who is in your organization, what roles they have, what they are allowed to touch. One place to add a user and have them exist everywhere — not three separate "please create an account in..." tickets.

✓

Your name service

Your own authoritative DNS for your infrastructure. Your printers, servers, cameras, and workstations all have names you control — not names dictated by an outside authority or a cloud vendor's portal.

✓

Your certificate authority

Every encrypted connection inside your infrastructure is signed by your certificate authority — not a public one with its own rules, rate limits, and change cycles. Rotate, revoke, re-issue whenever you need to.

✓

Your policy

The rules about who can do what, where, when. Written once, enforced everywhere. A policy change in SDNS updates every subordinate system — your firewalls, your switches, your VMs, your backups — in one coordinated push.

✓

Your audit log

One tamper-evident record of every action across every system. When the auditor asks "who changed this firewall rule on Tuesday?", you answer in seconds — not by grepping through four different log systems.

✓

Your coordinated updates

When one thing needs to change — a password reset, a key rotation, an emergency block — it happens everywhere at once, consistently. No drift. No stale records. No "I thought you did it."

Who is SDNS for?

The IT director of a 300-person company

You already run three DEC-LLC products, and they're starting to feel like three products.

NIVMIA knows about your switches. IVMIA knows about your VMs. OpenUTM knows about your firewall. But nobody knows about all of them. You want one view, one login, one place to grant access, one place to see what's happening. SDNS is that single place — and when you add VaultSync next quarter, it fits in without ceremony.

Outcome: Your 4 products act like one system, from one screen, with one audit trail.
The small business that just got a compliance requirement

An auditor is coming. You need to show control.

Who has admin access? Who logged in last week? Whose password expired? Which certificates expire this quarter? Which firewall rules changed in the last 30 days? Today those answers live in five places. With SDNS they live in one, and the report runs itself.

Outcome: You hand the auditor a single export instead of a scavenger hunt.
The school district with a tiny IT team

You cannot afford to log into six systems to add a new teacher.

Today, onboarding a new employee means: create a Microsoft account, create a VPN account, add them to the firewall, give them a wifi certificate, put them in the HR system, and hope nobody forgets a step. SDNS makes it one action — "add user, role = Teacher" — and every downstream system gets the update automatically.

Outcome: What took 40 minutes per new hire takes 60 seconds.
The nonprofit that lost its cloud budget

Microsoft/Google/AWS raised prices. You need to run on what you own.

You still need directory services, name services, certificate management, coordinated updates, audit logs — the things you used to get by paying someone else. SDNS gives you those capabilities on a server you own, in a closet you control, with no monthly invoice and no vendor rolling out features that break your workflow at their convenience.

Outcome: You replaced a five-figure annual cloud bill with a one-time appliance.
